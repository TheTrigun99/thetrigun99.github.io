## Latent Diffusion Models

To tackle the speed and sampling problem of DDPM, we tried to reduce the number of timesteps which works, but maybe we can improve things elsewhere too.  
Let's first understand where the compute is actually spent.

### First problem

First, our model works mostly on high-frequency details (HF), pixel-wise. Such accurate work is useless from a human point of view since these details are mostly imperceptible.

Now why does our model work mostly on HF details?  
Remember that our original loss is the ELBO, which minimizes a KL divergence. The thing is: if $p_{\text{data}} > 0$ and $p_{\theta} = 0$, the model is penalized — so it has to cover the entire support of $p_{\text{data}}$ (we call that property **<span style="color:red">mode-covering</span>**).  
See this [post](https://sander.ai/2020/09/01/typicality.html) for a more precise explanation.

Now, the higher the dimension, the larger the typical set where our image $I$ lives. The problem is that imperceptible variations $I'$ of our image also lie in the typical set, so $p_{\text{data}}(I') > 0$ for all these imperceptible changes — and thus the model has to assign $p_{\theta}(I') > 0$ too, even though for us $I' = I$.

**<span style="color:green">Our model then wastes compute on imperceptible changes.</span>**

Connecting this to the DDPM/DDIM timestep structure: during the forward process, high-frequency content is destroyed first (white Gaussian noise dominates the low-power high-frequency components quickly), while the semantic/low-frequency structure persists much longer.  
Equivalently, in the reverse direction: at large $t$ the model reconstructs coarse semantic content, and at small $t$ it refines fine perceptual details.  
The reweighted **<span style="color:red">simplified loss</span>** partially addresses this by down-weighting the loss at small $t$, where the model learns imperceptible details.

### Second problem

The second problem is that we work in the pixel space, which is very high-dimensional and where the data distribution is very complex.  
The model has to learn to generate images pixel by pixel, which requires a lot of data and compute.

**<span style="color:red">Our model works in a very complex, unnecessarily large space.</span>** This isn't necessary given the manifold hypothesis (our data likely lie in a much lower-dimensional subspace), and is expensive since we compute large operations throughout the model during both sampling and training.

### Solution

Formally, our model actually goes through 2 stages:
- **Perceptual compression**: removes high-frequency details but learns little semantic variation (small timesteps)
- **Semantic compression**: learns the semantic and conceptual composition of the data (larger timesteps)

The interesting part for our diffusion model is to focus compute on semantic compression, not on perceptual compression.

Latent Diffusion Models tackle this by first training an autoencoder to handle the perceptual compression, transporting our data from pixel space into a simpler and lower-dimensional <span style="color:green">**latent space**</span> where we then perform diffusion.

The autoencoder is more pertinent for perceptual compression:
- It doesn't suffer from **mode-covering** (it just needs to reconstruct, not cover the full distribution)
- It learns only pertinent representations (solves problem 1)

The latent space is lower-dimensional, so our diffusion model works on a considerably reduced space (factor 4 in our case with MNIST) and saves a lot of compute (solves problem 2).

Following the Latent Diffusion paper, our autoencoder is a **PatchGAN** (*Question: why is a PatchGAN pertinent here?*).

## Autoencoder

You have to be cautious training a LDM, because there is a lot to take into account, let's start with the autoencoder.

Our autoencoder learns the identity in a compressed form: it compresses the image (encoder) then decompresses it (decoder) to recover the original image.  
If the autoencoder learns a poor representation of the input space, the diffusion model won't do its job well either, since it won't be performing correct semantic compression.  

The pipeline is: encode the input → perform diffusion in latent space → decode the result.  

The encoder is basically downsampling blocks, the decoder upsampling blocks, and the PatchGAN discriminator is a simple ConvNet that operates on patches of the image rather than the full image.
